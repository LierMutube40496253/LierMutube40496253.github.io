Technology & Crime  ·  Twenty Years of Data

When the Machine
Changed the Crime

Three of the sharpest shifts in San Francisco crime over two decades were not driven by policing. They were driven by technology — an engine chip, a smartphone, a law aimed at a website.

2003 – 2024 · SFPD Incident Reports · ~3 million incidents

This page analyses crime data from San Francisco covering the period between 2003 and 2024. The dataset is based on SFPD police incident reports and contains detailed information on each recorded incident: crime type, location, date, time, and more. It is worth noting upfront that this data reflects reported incidents only — changes in enforcement priorities or reporting behaviour can look exactly like changes in crime itself, and that distinction will matter a great deal by the end of this story.

After working through this data, I noticed that three of the sharpest shifts across two decades share something unexpected: they appear to be driven by technological change. The crimes I will focus on are motor vehicle theft, larceny theft, and street prostitution. In the final chapter I expand the view to show more crime types alongside other technology-related events, so you can look for connections I may have missed.

01 Three Crimes, Three Technologies

Motor Vehicle Theft

California's 2005 law required all new cars to include engine immobilizers — a chip that prevents the engine starting without the correct electronic key, making hotwiring essentially obsolete. The drop in the data begins almost immediately.

Some of the remaining thefts are likely older vehicles without this system. It is also worth noting that the law was only possible because the technology already existed and had been standardised — the legislation was the trigger, not the invention.

Larceny Theft

Roughly the opposite happened here. Smartphones crossed 50% US penetration around 2011–2012, placing a high-value, pocket-sized device in almost everyone's hands — with limited tracking or resale prevention at the time. Apple's Activation Lock (iOS 7, 2013) was a partial countermeasure, but users found workarounds regularly. Then COVID-19 emptied the streets in 2020.

Street Prostitution

The drop begins earlier than the enforcement laws often cited. Around 2011, free content platforms began to dominate — with 80–90% of users accessing content at no cost — and the early decline is consistent with that shift in demand.

Then in 2018, FOSTA-SESTA targeted platforms hosting sex work advertising. Incidents fell to near zero. But this almost certainly does not mean the activity disappeared — it means it became invisible to the data.

02 Does Technology Affect All Neighbourhoods Equally?

District Ratios

The previous chapter showed how technology changed total crime numbers across San Francisco. Here I want to ask whether those changes were felt equally across districts — and whether income level played a role in who benefited from the new protections.

Each map shows the ratio of each crime per district compared to the city-wide average. Red (ratio > 1) means that district is overrepresented. Blue (ratio < 1) means the opposite. Use the animation controls to watch how ratios shift, and the tabs above each map to switch between crime types.

What to look for
In the motor vehicle theft map, watch the Mission district. Even as the overall count falls city-wide, its ratio rises — suggesting that newer security technology was adopted faster in areas with newer cars, concentrating the remaining theft where older vehicles were still common.

For larceny, theft is more evenly spread and in some cases higher in wealthier districts — which makes sense since richer areas have more valuable assets and more foot traffic in public spaces.

A note on interpretation
Technologies like smartphones were broadly accessible across income levels. But car security technology depends on whether you own a newer vehicle — a different story. The Mission ratios are consistent with that hypothesis, even if the data alone cannot confirm it.

03 Look for Yourself

Explore

In this final chapter I want to hand the exploration over to you. The chart shows how several crime types have evolved over time, alongside technology events I believe are plausibly connected.

Click any crime in the legend to show or hide it. Use the dropdown to overlay a technology event. Some events connect to more than one crime line — the connections are yours to find.

I have put forward the connections I found most compelling, but there are certainly others. You might even think of an event I have not included and notice it aligns with something in the data. That is kind of the point.

My own reflection: technology can have a significant and direct impact on crime in both directions. Some inventions create new opportunities while others close them off, and both effects tend to persist until some response catches up — whether a security measure, a law, or something as blunt as a pandemic.

Artificial intelligence is the obvious next case. Have the people building these systems thought seriously about the consequences? Or are they focused on the revenue?
